Multilingualism in Higher Education

A critical realist perspective on the challenges and fallacies of multilingualism in South African universities

Key Finding

"Multilingualism in South African universities remains a fallacy due to systemic barriers, despite policies designed to promote African Indigenous Languages."

The Fallacy of Multilingualism

This paper explores the empirical, real, and actual trajectories in South African universities regarding multilingualism and decolonisation. It employs the critical realist approach to uncover these trajectories through reviewed literature.

It investigates whether policy is followed in implementing African indigenous languages (AILs) against the hegemony of English and Afrikaans in South African universities. The paper found that multilingualism is still an area that requires attention, even though ample legislation and policies were drafted to necessitate decolonised practices that foster it.

Emsley & Modiba, 2024
Critical Analysis Research

Critical Realist Framework

The study uses Bhaskar's critical realism framework which conceptualizes social reality as having three domains: the real, the actual, and the empirical.

The real domain comprises social structures and generating mechanisms that assign power and resources. These create events in the actual domain, which may be observed in the empirical domain.

This framework helps uncover the incongruities in implementing AILs in South African universities and argues that the use of multilingualism in HE is a misconception.

Policy vs. Implementation Gap

Language Policy in HE (LPHE) provides a framework that advances and supports the use of indigenous languages (ILs) in teaching and learning. It stipulates guidelines for developing, implementing and for monitoring policies that transform institutions.

However, universities fail to create multilingual environments. Reports show limitations in implementation include lack of incentives, lack of clear directives, lack of mechanisms that enforce implementation, and lack of alignment between language policy and curriculum.

DHET, 2020
Policy Analysis Implementation

Basic-Higher Education Disconnect

There is a significant disconnect between basic education and higher education systems in South Africa. Instead of continuously using Eurocentric languages as the language of instruction, which results in high failure rates, multilingual strategies must be centered on high-quality mother-tongue education.

The 2021 Progress in International Reading Literacy Study reported that 81% of Grade 4 learners in SA cannot read for meaning in any language, with African language schools recording a decline in reading, while English and Afrikaans schools did not.

International Reading Study, 2021
Education System Literacy

Digitalization Challenges

Digitalization does not per se bring about diversification in terms of communicative practices. The hegemony of English prevails also in the digital realm, and power structures of the analogue world are mirrored online.

The main barrier in the digital development of African languages has been the comparatively meagre availability of digital text corpora that enable algorithms to build useful tools and resources for education.

Speakers of AILs have been disproportionally disadvantaged through the monolingual bias ingrained in voice recognition and mobile learning technologies.

Roux, 2020; Makalela, 2021
Technology Digital Divide

Intellectualization of African Languages

Intellectualization involves implementing policy, publishing and researching glossaries, and developing terminology in and of African languages, as well as using information and communication technologies to do so.

While intellectualization seems to implicitly suggest an epistemic deficiency of African languages, it is precisely not about catching up in linguistic complexity but about addressing the material gap concerning African languages caused by years of neglect and lack of both corpus and status planning.

Only African epistemic legitimacy would propel African ideas, and effective education can only take place in the language that the learner is most familiar with.

Kaschula & Maseko, 2017
Language Development Epistemology
